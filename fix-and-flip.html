What is fix and flip financing?

Fix and flip loans — also called hard money or bridge loans — are short-term financing designed for investors who buy distressed or undervalued properties, renovate them, and sell for profit. Unlike conventional mortgages, these loans are based primarily on the property's after-repair value (ARV), not the borrower's income.

Terms are typically 12–18 months. Interest rates are higher than long-term financing, but speed and flexibility are the tradeoff. When a deal depends on closing in 10 days, that spread becomes irrelevant compared to the profit on the flip.

How fix and flip underwriting works

The lender cares about three numbers: purchase price, rehab budget, and ARV. The loan is sized as a percentage of ARV — typically 65–75% of after-repair value. That percentage covers both the purchase and the rehab budget, drawn down in construction draws as the work is completed.

Your credit score matters but isn't the primary driver. Experience helps — first-time flippers may face slightly lower LTV or higher reserves. But the deal always comes first.

Typical fix and flip terms

  • Loan amounts: $75,000 – $3M+
  • Max LTV: Up to 90% of purchase price
  • Max LTC: Up to 90% of total project cost
  • Max ARV: Up to 75% of after-repair value
  • Rehab financing: Up to 100% of rehab costs (draw-based)
  • Loan term: 12 months (extensions available)
  • Interest rate: 10–13% typical depending on deal and experience
  • Points: 2–3% origination
  • Min credit score: 650

Fix & Flip FAQ

Can first-time flippers get fix and flip loans?

Yes, with some adjustments. First-time flippers may see lower max LTV (75–80% vs 90%), slightly higher rates, and may need to show liquid reserves. The deal still needs to pencil. We'll be straight with you about whether it does.

How do rehab draws work?

Rehab funds are held in escrow and released in draws as work is completed. You submit a draw request with documentation, an inspector verifies the work, and funds are released — typically within 48–72 hours of approval.

What's the BRRRR strategy and can I use it?

BRRRR — Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat. You use a fix and flip loan to buy and renovate, then refinance into a DSCR loan once the property is stabilized and rented. We handle both sides of that transaction and can structure it from the beginning.

What if the property needs more work than expected?

Rehab budget overruns are common. Some lenders allow contingency draws built into the original loan. We always recommend building 10–15% contingency into your initial rehab budget.
